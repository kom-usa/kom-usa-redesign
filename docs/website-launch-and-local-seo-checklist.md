# KOM USA website launch and local SEO checklist

Updated: July 14, 2026

This is the plain-English launch plan for the KOM USA website. It separates what is already in the site, what still requires a business decision, and what should be reviewed by a qualified professional.

> This document is operational guidance, not legal advice. A Michigan business attorney should approve the public privacy policy, website terms, service agreements, warranty language, and any marketing-consent language before launch.

## The short version

KOM USA should present itself as one credible Metro Detroit company with two clear service paths: Maintenance and Construction. The site should make it easy to understand the work, see real proof, confirm the regional service area, and contact the correct team.

The local SEO strategy is now:

- State clearly that KOM USA serves all of Metro Detroit.
- Publish useful pages for selected major communities.
- Link those pages from the homepage and service-area hub.
- Keep every city page honest: it is a service-area page, not a separate office.
- Do not produce hundreds of city-and-service combinations with near-identical copy.

## What we should take from Mr. Handyman

Mr. Handyman is a useful reference because the site repeatedly answers five customer questions: Are you local? Do you handle my project? Can I trust you? What happens next? How do I contact you?

### Header and navigation

Keep:

- One obvious service-request button visible in the header.
- A phone option that remains easy to find.
- Services grouped into understandable families instead of one enormous flat list.
- A dedicated Service Areas link.

KOM USA's current header already does this well with separate Maintenance and Construction navigation. Do not add Mr. Handyman's ZIP-code modal; KOM USA serves one metro area, so a simple address or ZIP field in the request flow is clearer.

### Homepage

Use this order:

1. Clear promise: one local team for Maintenance and Construction.
2. Real project photograph and direct request buttons.
3. Google rating and real review excerpts.
4. Maintenance versus Construction choice.
5. Popular services.
6. Why choose KOM USA, using claims the company can prove.
7. How the request process works.
8. Real project gallery.
9. Metro Detroit service-area section with linked city pages.
10. Short request form and useful FAQs.

Mr. Handyman repeats calls to action frequently. KOM USA should also give the customer a next step after major sections, but it should keep the current calmer layout and avoid a request form after every block.

### Individual service pages

Every important service page should answer:

- What problem does this service solve?
- What is normally included?
- What is not included or requires a different scope?
- What information or photos should the customer provide?
- How is pricing confirmed?
- What related services might be useful?
- What happens after the request?

The current KOM USA service catalog already supports this structure. The next improvement is to add genuine before-and-after examples to the highest-value service pages as enough project evidence becomes available.

### Local pages

The new city pages include original local context, relevant service links, a project request path, nearby community links, and structured data for the city served. They never claim that KOM USA has an office in that city.

This is important because Google's service-area-business rules generally allow one profile for the real central operation unless separate locations have separate staff and meet Google's real-location requirements. Virtual offices are not valid locations. See [Google's Business Profile guidelines](https://support.google.com/business/answer/3038177) and [service-area guidance](https://support.google.com/business/answer/9157481).

Do next:

- Add one or two real projects to a city page when KOM USA has permission to identify the city.
- Add a city-specific customer quote only when the review is real and its source is retained.
- Review city pages every six months for accuracy.
- Add another city only when it is truly served and there is enough useful content to justify a page.

Avoid:

- Fake addresses, virtual offices, or extra Google Business Profiles for city pages.
- Pages that change only the city name.
- Claims such as “same-day,” “licensed,” “insured,” “guaranteed,” or “10 years of experience” until the business can document and consistently honor them.
- Copying competitor headings, page copy, graphics, layouts, guarantees, or policy text.

### Trust and proof

Mr. Handyman benefits from a national guarantee, uniforms, marked vans, years-of-experience claims, and many reviews. KOM USA should build its own proof system:

- Real KOM USA project photography only.
- Current Google rating and linked source.
- Clear legal business name and contact information.
- Insurance and license statements only after documents are verified and the exact applicable work is understood.
- Written workmanship or warranty promise only after the operations team and attorney approve what it covers, exclusions, reporting window, and remedy.
- A simple “How we work” page after the business process is finalized.

### Footer

The footer should contain:

- Maintenance and Construction links.
- Projects, Service Areas, About, Blog, and Contact.
- Both phone numbers and the company email.
- “Serving all of Metro Detroit.”
- Copyright notice using the legal owner.
- Privacy Policy and Website Terms.
- An Accessibility link after an accessibility statement and feedback process are approved.

## Legal and policy setup: how to make it official

### 1. Copyright

Copyright protection generally begins when original work is created and fixed; registration is a separate step that creates important enforcement benefits. The footer notice does not register the website. The U.S. Copyright Office has a specific guide for websites and website content in [Circular 66](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ66.pdf) and an official [registration portal](https://copyright.gov/registration/).

Checklist:

- [ ] Confirm that KOM Construction LLC owns or has a written license for every logo, photograph, video, illustration, and block of copy.
- [ ] Get written assignment or license language from photographers, designers, employees, and independent contractors where needed.
- [ ] Keep the original files, invoices, release forms, and contracts in a company-controlled folder.
- [ ] Keep `© [current year] KOM Construction LLC. All rights reserved.` in the footer.
- [ ] Ask an intellectual-property attorney whether registering the site's text, photography, logo artwork, or major updates is worthwhile.
- [ ] If registering, use copyright.gov directly or have the attorney file it. Do not pay a look-alike “registration service” without checking it carefully.

### 2. Privacy Policy

A privacy policy becomes the company's official policy when the business verifies that it accurately describes real practices, approves it, publishes it, and follows it. A template cannot discover what the company actually collects or how vendors use it.

Checklist:

- [x] Create a working policy that describes the current site forms and providers.
- [ ] Confirm the legal owner name, business mailing address, and privacy contact email.
- [ ] Decide how long leads, estimates, form submissions, project photos, and inactive customer records are retained.
- [ ] Confirm which people and contractors can access Netlify, Brevo, Sanity, Google, email, and any future customer system.
- [ ] Sign or review vendor data-processing terms where available.
- [ ] Have a Michigan business/privacy attorney review the policy against the final vendor list and service process.
- [ ] Have an owner approve the dated version in writing and retain a PDF copy of each published version.
- [ ] Update the policy before adding advertising pixels, session replay, online payments, customer accounts, SMS marketing, or a new CRM.

The FTC's [small-business cybersecurity guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/small-businesses/cybersecurity) recommends knowing where customer data is stored, limiting vendor use, using HTTPS, maintaining software, protecting business email, and enabling strong access controls.

### 3. Website Terms of Use

Website terms govern use of the website. They are not a replacement for the estimate, work order, construction contract, payment terms, cancellation policy, warranty, or other project documents.

Checklist:

- [x] Add a conservative working draft covering website use and online requests.
- [ ] Have a Michigan construction/business attorney review the draft.
- [ ] Confirm that the no-booking language matches the actual request workflow.
- [ ] Confirm governing law, dispute language, warranty references, and limitations of liability with counsel.
- [ ] Keep project-specific legal terms in the signed estimate or service agreement—not only on the website.
- [ ] Publish the attorney-approved version with an effective date and retain the approved copy.

### 4. Service agreements, estimates, and warranties

The website cannot make these “official.” The business needs approved operating documents that the team actually uses.

- [ ] Maintenance work order or service agreement.
- [ ] Construction estimate and contract appropriate to the project type.
- [ ] Change-order process.
- [ ] Deposit, progress-payment, final-payment, and cancellation terms.
- [ ] Materials, permits, customer-supplied items, hidden conditions, access, cleanup, and delay language.
- [ ] Workmanship warranty with duration, exclusions, claim method, and remedy.
- [ ] Photo and testimonial release.
- [ ] Subcontractor agreement and proof-of-insurance process.
- [ ] Required Michigan license numbers and disclosures for the types of work actually offered.

Use a Michigan construction attorney for these documents. Do not copy a competitor's terms or contract.

### 5. Email and text messages

The current website says a service request is not a marketing signup. Keep transactional follow-up separate from marketing consent.

- [ ] If marketing email is added, use a separate unchecked opt-in.
- [ ] Include the sender's valid postal address and an unsubscribe method in commercial email.
- [ ] Process unsubscribe requests and maintain a suppression list.
- [ ] If marketing texts or automated calls are added, have counsel and the messaging provider approve the exact consent language, recordkeeping, STOP/HELP handling, quiet hours, and campaign setup before launch.

The FTC's [CAN-SPAM compliance guide](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business) explains requirements for commercial email, including accurate sender information, a postal address, opt-out instructions, and honoring opt-outs.

### 6. Accessibility

The Department of Justice says businesses open to the public must make their online services accessible, while existing technical standards such as WCAG provide useful guidance. Use WCAG 2.2 Level AA as the technical target. See [ADA.gov web accessibility guidance](https://www.ada.gov/resources/web-guidance/) and the [W3C WCAG overview](https://www.w3.org/WAI/standards-guidelines/wcag/).

- [ ] Complete automated accessibility checks on every page template.
- [ ] Test keyboard-only navigation, visible focus, menus, forms, errors, and skip links.
- [ ] Test representative pages with VoiceOver on macOS/iPhone.
- [ ] Confirm text contrast, zoom at 200%, responsive reflow, alt text, headings, and form labels.
- [ ] Caption video and provide transcripts where appropriate.
- [ ] Publish a short accessibility statement with a monitored contact method.
- [ ] Retest after major design, navigation, form, or CMS changes.

## Beginner-friendly launch checklist

### Business facts and ownership

- [ ] Confirm the public brand name and legal company name.
- [ ] Confirm both phone numbers, company email, service-request response expectations, and hours.
- [ ] Confirm the real Metro Detroit coverage and any jobs or distances that are not accepted.
- [ ] Decide whether the business address is public. Do not publish a home address only for SEO.
- [ ] Verify licenses, insurance, and bond information before making those claims.
- [ ] Confirm who owns the domain, Netlify site, Sanity project, Google Business Profile, Google Search Console, email system, and social accounts.
- [ ] Require multi-factor authentication and at least two trusted business administrators for critical accounts.

### Content and proof

- [ ] Read every page for factual accuracy.
- [ ] Confirm every service shown is currently offered.
- [ ] Confirm every project photograph belongs to KOM USA or is licensed.
- [ ] Obtain permission for identifiable customers, addresses, license plates, documents, and private interiors.
- [ ] Verify every testimonial against its source and keep the source record.
- [ ] Remove temporary prices, placeholder offers, or availability promises.
- [ ] Check that city pages do not claim separate offices.

### Forms and customer handling

- [ ] Submit a real test through every form on desktop and mobile.
- [ ] Confirm the form reaches the correct inbox or system.
- [ ] Confirm the customer receives the intended acknowledgement.
- [ ] Confirm spam protection works without blocking a normal request.
- [ ] Test error messages, required fields, back/forward steps, and the thank-you page.
- [ ] Document who checks new requests, how often, who calls back, and what happens when the first person is unavailable.
- [ ] Never collect Social Security numbers, payment-card data, medical information, or other sensitive information in the general project-description field.

### SEO and local visibility

- [ ] Verify one accurate Google Business Profile as a service-area business unless additional real staffed locations qualify.
- [ ] Use the exact real business name—do not add city or service keywords to the profile name.
- [ ] Add up to 20 accurate service areas in Google Business Profile.
- [ ] Keep the website, profile, directories, insurance documents, and real-world branding consistent.
- [ ] Verify Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools.
- [ ] Submit the production sitemap.
- [ ] Check that every important page has one H1, a unique title, a useful description, and a canonical URL.
- [ ] Check redirects from all Wix or older website URLs before changing the domain.
- [ ] Request indexing for the homepage, main service hubs, service-area hub, and strongest city pages after launch.
- [ ] Review Search Console monthly for indexing, security, and structured-data problems.

### Technical launch

- [ ] Run the production build with no errors.
- [ ] Crawl the built site for broken internal links and missing images.
- [ ] Test Chrome, Safari, Firefox, iPhone, and Android-sized screens.
- [ ] Test the sticky header, mobile menu, phone links, city links, forms, and footer links.
- [ ] Confirm HTTPS, the primary domain, and `www` behavior.
- [ ] Confirm DNS records, Google Workspace email records, SPF, DKIM, and DMARC before changing nameservers.
- [ ] Back up the current production site and export any content that must be retained.
- [ ] Confirm the custom 404 page returns a real 404 status.
- [ ] Confirm `robots.txt` and the sitemap use the final production domain.
- [ ] Keep previews and drafts out of search results.
- [ ] Set uptime monitoring and an expiring-domain reminder.

### Privacy and security

- [ ] Finish attorney review of Privacy Policy, Website Terms, and service agreements.
- [ ] Add the business mailing address where legally or operationally required.
- [ ] Document the vendors that receive customer data.
- [ ] Limit account access and remove former users promptly.
- [ ] Store recovery codes in a company-controlled password manager.
- [ ] Decide the lead and customer record-retention schedule.
- [ ] Create a simple incident plan: who to call, how to preserve evidence, how to disable a compromised account, and who evaluates notification duties.
- [ ] Do not add analytics, advertising, or session recording until the privacy policy and consent approach are updated.

### Final go-live day

- [ ] Freeze content changes for the launch window.
- [ ] Run one final build and link check.
- [ ] Take screenshots of the homepage, a service page, service-area hub, city page, request form, Privacy Policy, and Website Terms.
- [ ] Publish.
- [ ] Test the production domain from a phone using cellular data.
- [ ] Send a new form request and reply to it end to end.
- [ ] Check Search Console, analytics if approved, uptime monitoring, and error logs.
- [ ] Keep the previous deployment available for rollback.
